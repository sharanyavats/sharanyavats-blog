Why I'm Building CarbonLens

December 22, 2025 | By Sharanya Vats

I'm a high school sophomore who has spent several hours so far building CarbonLens - a tool that calculates how much CO₂ your AI usage generates.

The Problem

Last month, I learned that training GPT-3 generated 552 tons of CO₂. That's equivalent to driving 1.2 million miles.

But here's what nobody talks about: What about USING AI every day?

Billions of people use ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney. Every query, every image generation, every conversation consumes energy and generates emissions. What's the cumulative impact of all those queries?

I couldn't find a simple answer. Most research focuses on training costs, not inference costs. So I decided to build something myself.

The Solution

CarbonLens makes AI's environmental impact visible and understandable.

Here's how it works:

1. You tell it which AI tool you used (ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, etc.)
2. You tell it how many minutes you used it
3. It calculates your CO₂ emissions
4. It shows equivalents you can understand - phone charges, driving distance, trees needed to offset

Version 1 is simple. It's not perfect. But it works.

What I Learned

Building CarbonLens taught me several things:

First, the data is surprisingly hard to find. While training emissions are well-documented, inference emissions vary wildly based on model architecture, hardware, and grid carbon intensity.

Second, the scale is staggering. If ChatGPT's 100 million users each spent just 10 minutes per day using it, that's roughly 50 tons of CO₂ daily - or 18,000 tons per year. And that's just one AI tool.

Third, people care when they can see the impact. Abstract numbers like "0.5g per minute" don't resonate. But "equivalent to charging 6 smartphones" does.

What's Next

This is just Version 1. I'm planning to add:

• User accounts to track emissions over time
• More AI tools and more accurate data
• Chrome extension for automatic tracking
• Comparison tools to see how different models compare
• Integration with actual power grid data for real-time carbon intensity

My ultimate goal: Make every AI user aware of their environmental impact and empower them to make more sustainable choices.

The Bigger Picture

I'm a sophomore planning to study computational environmental science. I want to work at NASA as an environmental scientist for space exploration.

CarbonLens connects to this mission: Whether analyzing AI's water consumption on Earth or studying radiation in space, we need to understand the environmental impacts of our technology.

This is my first step in that direction.
